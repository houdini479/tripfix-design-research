Overview

Executive Summary

TripFix is transitioning from a paralegal-driven claims management tool to an AI-first platform. This is a fundamental shift: from the user as the worker to the user as the quality controller and strategic decision-maker.

Core Design Principle

Verification-first design: The interface must make it cheap for a reviewer to answer: "Is this correct, and can I prove it?" Every AI recommendation should point to the exact evidence that supports it, and visibly flag when evidence is insufficient. 8

This interactive report compiles research across legal technology, insurance claims automation, software development review tools, data labelling platforms, content moderation systems, cybersecurity, medical imaging, fraud detection, and emerging AI UX pattern libraries.

The research converges on six critical design domains, each explored in depth below with embedded product examples, videos, and design patterns that the team should study.

Section 1

Operating Model & Design Constraints

Before diving into interaction patterns, TripFix must reckon with the constraints that make legal claims work fundamentally different from generic AI-assisted workflows.

1.1 The Reviewer Is Not Editing a Draft

Reviewers are performing quality assurance under time pressure 9, often creating an evidentiary record that must stand up to escalation, dispute, or audit. The UI must make it easy to:

  1. See what the AI did
  2. Verify against source evidence
  3. Correct efficiently
  4. Justify and annotate decisions
  5. Prove accountability through traceability

1.2 Regulatory & Professional Constraints

  • Professional responsibility: Legal professionals remain accountable for work product (Law Society of Ontario, ABA, Canadian Bar Association) 10
  • Privacy frameworks: PIPEDA and GDPR impact how AI prompts, outputs, and audit logs are stored
  • AI governance standards: NIST AI RMF, ISO 42001, EU AI Act push toward explicit risk mapping and measurable controls 11

1.3 Participation Models: HITL, HOTL, and Hybrid

TripFix needs a hybrid model: Human-in-the-Loop (HITL) for complex liability assessments and novel claim types, Human-over-the-Loop (HOTL) for routine data extraction and administrative filing. The system should clearly indicate which mode applies to each claim and why.

Design Implication

TripFix's UX must be configurable‚Äîpolicy rules, templates, routing thresholds, and audit settings‚Äîrather than hard-coded. Jurisdictions, claim volumes, SLAs, and evidence types will vary without requiring engineering changes.

Section 2

Workflow Automation & AI Handoffs

When AI handles 80-90% of claims, the UX must shift from "here's your to-do list" to "here's what needs your expert judgment."

2.1 Exception-Based Processing

The dominant pattern across legal tech and claims automation is exception-based review 12. AI handles the straightforward work end-to-end; humans focus on exceptions‚Äîclaims that are ambiguous, high-risk, or where AI confidence is low.

Key Insight ‚Äî Straight-Through Processing (STP)

Modern insurance claims systems achieve 65%+ STP rates 13, meaning the majority of claims resolve without human intervention. TripFix should target a tiered model: auto-resolved claims, flagged-for-review claims, and escalated-to-specialist claims.

2.2 Tiered Autonomy Model

The emerging best practice maps autonomy to confidence and risk, not applying uniform review depth to every claim:

Tier 1 ‚Äî Full Automation

Auto-Complete

Routine data extraction, deadline calculations, document formatting. Auto-completed with optional sampling QC.

Tier 2 ‚Äî AI Draft + Spot Check

Summary Review

Standard claims where AI generates the full work product and a paralegal reviews a summary. Confirm facts, eligibility, and outbound comms.

Tier 3 ‚Äî AI Assist + Full Review

Expert Review

Complex, high-value, or novel claims. AI provides evidence gathering and recommendations; paralegal works through full analysis. Senior lawyer sign-off required.

2.3 Confidence-Based Routing

Confidence must be operationalised as routing policy, not displayed as decoration. Thresholds at two layers:

  • Claim-level tier: Overall routing to auto-approve, needs-review, needs-senior-audit, or needs-compliance-attention
  • Sub-task-level gates: Per extraction field, per legal rule application, per draft clause, per redaction decision
Calibration Insight

Confidence is only useful if calibrated‚Äîa score of 0.9 should mean approximately 90% likely correct. Poorly calibrated confidence misleads reviewers. TripFix should implement reliability diagrams and recalibrate after major model updates.

Section 3

Dashboard & Queue Design

When the paralegal's primary role shifts to reviewing AI work, the dashboard becomes a command centre. It must answer three questions: What needs my attention? How is the system performing? Are there patterns I should know about?

3.1 Three-Zone Dashboard

Based on the research, TripFix's dashboard should be organised into three zones:

üî¥ Action Zone

Claims needing human judgment now. Sorted by urgency and impact. Displays AI confidence, specific flag reason, and estimated review time. Supports bulk selection.

üîµ Monitoring Zone

Real-time pipeline overview. Claims being processed, completion rates, average confidence, STP rate trends, and SLA status. The ops manager's primary view.

üü¢ Audit Zone

Searchable history of all completed claims‚Äîboth auto-resolved and human-reviewed‚Äîfor compliance review, QC sampling, and pattern analysis.

3.2 Agent Oversight Patterns

UX researcher B.P. Rigent documented seven patterns for human oversight of ambient AI agents. Three are directly applicable to TripFix:

  • Overview Panel: Real-time agent status (idle, running, paused, needs attention) at a glance
  • Oversight Flow: How humans resolve tasks requiring attention through approve/reject/modify workflows
  • Event Stream Configuration: Customisable triggers for what requires human involvement vs. auto-resolves

3.3 Smart Triage & Prioritisation

The queue should be intelligently prioritised, not a flat chronological list. Key patterns:

  • Complexity indicators: Each claim displays classification (simple/medium/complex), confidence level, estimated review time, and specific flags
  • Turn-based attention cues: Clear "what requires my attention next" signals prevent dashboard thrash (documented in Gerrit Code Review's attention set pattern)
  • Policy-based routing: Route by privilege indicators, novel jurisdiction, high monetary value, contradictory evidence, or compliance flags

Section 4

Review & Feedback UX

The review experience is where quality and efficiency intersect. The UX must enable expert-level review while silently capturing feedback that improves AI performance.

4.1 The Review Triad Layout

A high-throughput review workbench should consistently present three regions:

Queue + Triage (Left)

"What should I do next?" with filters, risk tiers, and attention signals.

Claim Narrative + Decisions (Centre)

AI summary with citations, editable eligibility decision, compensation inputs, draft letter in diff view, and action buttons: Approve / Request Changes / Escalate / Defer.

Claim History (Right)

Unified timeline of all evidence, AI reasoning, and human actions. Every event in the claim's lifecycle ‚Äî one scrollable, searchable, source-linked view.

Ergonomic rule: Keep the default view "just enough to decide" but allow deep dives without leaving the page (progressive disclosure).

4.2 Structured Suggestion Blocks

Rather than free-form AI text, each suggestion should be a structured block containing:

  • Proposed value with confidence indicator
  • Evidence links supporting the value
  • One-click actions: Accept, Edit, Reject, Needs More Evidence
  • Reject requires a reason code (fast dropdown + optional comment) to feed analytics

4.3 Approval Grammar

Adopt a two-state approval model borrowed from code review:

  • Approve: "I affirm this artefact meets standard."
  • Request Changes: "AI output is directionally correct but needs edits before approval."

Approvals should be scoped: reviewers may approve "fact extraction" but require a senior lawyer for "final demand letter wording." Multi-step approvals without full re-review.

4.4 Batch Processing with Guardrails

Batch review minimises human cost per claim‚Äîif coupled with guardrails:

  • Queue segmenting: Group by confidence bands and policy rules
  • Bulk actions: "Approve selected," "Apply template," "Escalate selected," plus "Undo last bulk" and "Preview impact"
  • Built-in spot-checking: "Approve 50 claims, sample 5 to verify first." Ops manager controls sampling rate by risk tier.

4.5 Passive Feedback Collection

The most effective feedback systems are built into the workflow, not bolted on 15:

The paralegal never thinks about "training the AI." They're doing their job, and the system captures the signal. This is how EvenUp, Labelbox, and GitHub Copilot all approach feedback.

Cross-Industry Insight ‚Äî HackerOne's "Shadowing" Model

In cybersecurity code review, AI agents "shadow" human experts as they work, creating long-term memories about architecture, institutional knowledge, and team policies. Over time, the AI replicates expert judgment patterns. For TripFix, the system should learn airline-specific patterns, jurisdictional idiosyncrasies, and firm-level preferences from reviewer corrections.

Section 5

Trust, Transparency & Explainability

If paralegals don't trust the AI, they over-review everything (defeating efficiency). If they over-trust, quality drops. The goal is calibrated trust. 14

5.1 Confidence Visualisation ‚Äî Traffic Light Model

Present confidence as a chip with tooltip including calibration note, top factors/evidence links, and risk tier:

85+
High Confidence
Output likely correct. Minimal review required.
60+
Medium Confidence
Subtly prompts human review. Check flagged fields.
<60
Low Confidence
Demands human action. AI should abstain and defer.

For TripFix, show confidence at multiple levels: per-field (e.g., "passenger name: 98%" vs. "compensation amount: 72%"), per-section, and per-claim overall.

Radiology UX Paradox

In medical imaging research, 80% of radiologists preferred complex interfaces with overlays and confidence scores, yet their accuracy improved most with simple text-only output. 16 Lesson for TripFix: test which confidence presentations improve reviewer accuracy rather than assuming more visual detail is better.

5.2 Reasoning Trails & Explainability

In legal work, confidence scores alone are insufficient. Professionals need to understand why the AI reached a conclusion. In TripFix, every AI reasoning step feeds into the Claim History timeline ‚Äî a single, unified audit trail.

Harvey's Paper Trail Design

Harvey AI explicitly designs for auditability: they surface a clear paper trail of thinking steps and citations. Users can backtrack through logic, verify sources, confirm citation accuracy. Design philosophy: "not a black box." 18

Shape of AI: Stream of Thought Pattern

The Shape of AI pattern library documents the "Stream of Thought" pattern: a bounded box showing the AI's logic via three expressions:

  • Human-readable plans previewing what the AI will do
  • Execution logs recording tool calls and results
  • Compact reasoning summaries capturing decisions

Full Library ‚Üó Stream of Thought ‚Üó Governors ‚Üó

Created by Emily Campbell, Shape of AI catalogs patterns across six categories: Wayfinders, Inputs, Tuners, Governors (human oversight), Trust Builders, and Identifiers. The Governors category is most relevant to TripFix.

Products using Stream of Thought include: ChatGPT (thinking tasks), Perplexity (step-by-step sources), Lovable (action/reasoning trace), and V0 (inline logic during code generation).

Design considerations: Treat steps as clear states (queued, running, completed, error). Tailor visibility to context complexity. Separate plan/execution/evidence views. Show plans before acting.

5.3 Trust Calibration Over Time

The "Trust Ladder" framework describes systems progressing from Tier 0 (experimental) through higher tiers as they demonstrate reliability. The most effective technique:

Trust-Building Formula

"For EU261 delay claims under 3 hours, the AI has 97% accuracy over the last 6 months" is far more trust-building than a generic percentage. 17 Show the track record, not just the prediction. TripFix should build a feedback loop dashboard showing AI performance by claim type, complexity, and time period.

Section 6

Claim History ‚Äî The Single Source of Truth

Evidence, AI reasoning, and human decisions belong in one unified timeline. Claim History is the single place an admin goes to answer: "What happened with this claim, and why?"

Core Design Principle

One claim, one timeline. Rather than splitting evidence, AI reasoning, and audit logs into separate tabs or admin panels, Claim History merges them into a single chronological view. Every event ‚Äî document uploaded, AI analysis run, human override, system routing decision ‚Äî appears in one scrollable, searchable, source-linked timeline. 1

6.1 What Belongs in the Timeline

Claim History unifies three previously siloed views into one:

üìé Evidence

Every document, email, photo, or data point that entered the claim. Timestamped, tagged by source, with inline viewer links.

ü§ñ AI Reasoning

Each AI analysis run: model version, conclusion, confidence scores, cited evidence, and the full reasoning chain ‚Äî Harvey's "Paper Trail" approach. 2

üë§ Human Actions

Every reviewer action: approvals, edits (with diffs), rejections with reason codes, escalations, comments, and sign-offs ‚Äî with who, when, and why.

6.3 Provenance Data Model

The Claim History timeline is backed by a provenance data model (inspired by W3C PROV: entity/activity/agent 5) that structures every event:

  • CLAIM ‚Üí EVIDENCE_ITEM: What evidence exists for this claim
  • CLAIM ‚Üí AI_RUN ‚Üí AI_OUTPUT ‚Üí AI_CITATION: What the AI produced and cited
  • CLAIM ‚Üí REVIEW_TASK ‚Üí REVIEW_ACTION ‚Üí OUTPUT_VERSION: What the human reviewed and changed
  • AUDIT_EVENT: Immutable log entries linking users, actions, timestamps, and artefacts
  • POLICY_RULE ‚Üí REVIEW_TASK: What policy drove the routing decision
Why This Matters

Every event in the Claim History is immutable and timestamped. AI runs record inputs, model version, and policies applied. Human actions record approve/reject/edit decisions with reason codes. This means the Claim History is the audit trail and the compliance record ‚Äî it's not a separate system or a hidden admin panel. 6

6.4 Privilege, Redaction & Retention

  • Redaction tools: Expose redaction in the review workflow. E-discovery tools like Relativity provide manual and rule-based redaction with reviewer-friendly markup.
  • Retention controls: Reviewers need visibility into retention class and sensitivity level.
  • Privilege handling: Flag potentially privileged communications before they reach outbound artefacts.

Implementation Roadmap

Immediate design priorities and an illustrative timeline assuming a start in late February 2026.

Immediate Design Priorities

  1. Task decomposition workshop: Enumerate every claim sub-task AI will output. Map each to evidence sources and risk tiers. Define evidence-tethered vs. assistive only.
  2. Define autonomy tiers: Map every claim type and processing step to Tier 1/2/3. This taxonomy drives every other UX decision.
  3. Design the review triad: Build a prototype of the three-pane workbench. Test with paralegals using real claim data.
  4. Implement confidence scoring: Start with three-tier model. Show historical accuracy alongside confidence. Test which presentations improve reviewer accuracy.
  5. Instrument from day one: Implement the provenance model early. Retrofitting compromises defensibility.

Prototype Approach

Design two prototypes in parallel:

  • Prototype A: Asynchronous queue-first workbench with batch processing (recommended default for legal review)
  • Prototype B: Real-time AI assist inside drafting for outbound communications

Compare via time-per-claim and error outcomes with gold-set review studies.
